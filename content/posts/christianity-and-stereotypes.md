"Wow, you don't sound very Christian", "Ohh, so you don't really believe in God" -- I sometimes get this remark from my friends or colleagues, in response to something I had done or said. Often, I blushed in shame, knowing I had shamed God. I strived to be God's perfect son, adhering to the Torah laws and His commandments. I displayed compassion, showed love and patience (or at least tried to), all the while watching my conduct and manner of speech. But the chasm between outward facade and inner heart grows deeper and wider. The things which I want to do I do not do. The things which I do not want to do I should do. Perhaps this is the absurdity depicted by Camus in his book *The Myth of Sisyphus*.

Was my heart lacking? Was the outward display really a manifestation of my inward disbelief? Where did I go wrong?

I often received feedback or comments that Christians are hypocrites. They wear a mask on their face daily, hiding their true feelings beneath a "holier than thou" disguise. This tetrachotomy between what one should do, one should not do, what one wants to do, and what one does not want to do, the force of tension pulling at each side is what I described as absurdity in the opening. It is the reason for the 'hypocrisy' viewed by the general mass of Christians and their behaviours; they don't practise what they preach.

Must we be fully transformed from within to the likeness of God in order to perform acts of goodness? Or must we only do what we feel in our heart? One day a friend told me about someone committing suicide. There were two choices in front of me: "Ohh, how his family grieves his passing", or "Wow, that took conviction". I said the latter. It revealed the state of my heart. Jesus would feel the pain. Most of us can't imagine the grief and pain of the situation which led to this suicide. There is a saying in Chinese: the joy and sadness between humans are, unfortunately, unrelatable most of the time.

To many non-believers (and even believers), Christianity seems like a list of todos and not todos. Do not steal. Do not covet. Honor your parents. The list goes on and on. In fact, this applies to other religions like Islam (where its Quran has some overlap with our Bible). It is true. Most proper religions have their own laws. Since ancient times, any form of proper civilization is shaped and ruled by a set of laws. The British had their own laws. The Romans had their laws. The Chinese had their own laws. They served to ensure the stability of society, protect its citizens, pass fair judgements on criminals, and maintain the interests of the majority of individuals (or the ruling party).

Worldly laws are important. But they draw the lowest boundary between what should not be done and what could be done. Worldly laws are confined to the physical realm. They do not protect the emotional or the spiritual being of individuals. The punishment for stealing is applicable only to physical items. I've not heard of someone being fined or jailed for stealing someone's heart.

Spiritual laws go one step further. They guide individuals to grow. Be compassionate. Show love to your neighbours. Be generous with what you have. Worldly laws protect the individual interest, keeping society from falling apart. Spiritual laws help us grow collectively as sons and daughters of God (basically society). By grow, here I mean spiritually, in conduct, in wisdom, in ethics, in morals.

One of the reasons people reject Christ is because they hate being told what to do, and what not to do. And they certainly dislike those who practise them (some of them, not all). They have the stereotypes planted in their minds that Christians are those who obey these laws strictly, and if they fail, they end up being labeled -- "he doesn't really believe", "he is the same as me".

People often miss the point of the Bible and its laws and stories. At its core, the whole message is about grace and mercy, and most importantly, love. Love is the center of the Gospel. It forms the crux of it. Without love there is no grace, there is no mercy, no Messiah, no redemption and salvation.

The law (from here on, I'm referring to the spiritual law) is the mirror which reflects the ugliness of our heart. Love and grace are what saves us. Because we know we can't fulfill them, we turn to His everlasting grace for redemption. That, my brothers and sisters, is Christianity.

Christians and non-Christians, all have fallen short of the glory of God. We sin alike. We covet alike. We lie alike. Knowingly and unknowingly. I've met non-Christians who are nicer and kinder than some Christians. Granted, we always strive to do our best, to achieve and attain perfection in these laws through the cycle of actions and reflections. Some are more 'holy' than others (by normal human standards). Others not so much. All in all, it is love which saves, and connects us all. We don't turn to Christ to fulfill these laws, we turn to Christ because we can't.

I do not identify myself as a staunch Christian. I held doubt. I questioned. I failed. Nevertheless, I believe this journey of seeking answers is what makes Christianity meaningful. Indeed, Christianity is a journey.

I hope this short writing sheds some light on the ingrained image of Christianity in the minds of the non-believers. For believers, may this aid in your spiritual maturity.

God bless you.
