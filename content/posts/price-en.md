I’m starting to think our boundaries don’t really show themselves before a choice is made. They only become visible after the cost starts landing on us for real.

Today I had coffee with a friend of a friend who works in private equity. Near the end, I asked him what advice he’d give me if he had to give just one.

He said maybe what a person needs first is a way of living that lets them be at peace with themselves. A life they can come home to, internally. Then he said something that stayed with me:

Maybe the better question isn’t what you want, but what you’re willing to pay for it.

I’ve been thinking about that ever since.

A while ago, someone asked me why I quit my job without anything lined up. I answered pretty honestly: I was tired of living like that.

Then they asked me what I wanted instead.

I said, after thinking for a second: money.

They laughed a little and said that if that was true, quitting was probably the worst thing I could have done.

And the uncomfortable thing was, they weren’t exactly wrong.

At least not by the usual logic. If you want money, then staying on a stable path, doing the sensible thing, keeping the income coming, that is supposed to be the answer. And it wasn’t some life that was obviously unbearable from the outside. It was respectable. It made sense on paper. It had structure, momentum, reward. It was exactly the kind of life people hesitate to leave, for good reason.

Later someone told me maybe money wasn’t actually what I wanted. Maybe I needed to think more carefully about what I really wanted from life.

That stayed with me too.

For a while I kept turning it over in my head. Maybe I didn’t want it badly enough. Maybe I kept saying I wanted money, but not in the way I thought I did. Maybe I was less driven than I wanted to believe.

But slowly I started to feel like I had been asking the wrong question the whole time.

Maybe the important thing was never just what I wanted. Maybe it was what I was actually willing to give in exchange for it.

Because wanting something is easy. Almost embarrassingly easy. Everyone wants the good life in whatever form it takes: money, love, health, freedom, meaning, beauty, calm, some version of fullness. Desire is not the hard part. The hard part is that life does not hand things over just because we want them. It asks something back.

And not in theory. In actual terms.

Time. Energy. Sleep. Certainty. Pride. Sometimes health. Sometimes your peace.

I realized the issue was never whether I wanted money.

Of course I do.

That’s why I was willing to work twelve-hour days. That’s why I kept going for as long as I did. But if getting more of it meant continuing to lose sleep, wear down my health, and live in a state that made me feel less and less like myself, then somewhere along the way I found my answer.

Not that I didn’t want money. Just that I didn’t want it that much.

Or maybe more accurately, I wanted it, but not enough to keep paying that particular price.

And I think that distinction matters.

Because sometimes we talk about limits as if they are evidence of weakness, lack of discipline, lack of hunger. But maybe sometimes a limit is just a truth you only discover by living into it. You don’t always know beforehand what you’re willing to sacrifice. You think you do. You make little speeches to yourself about ambition and resilience and trade-offs. But it’s only when desire and cost arrive together, with their full weight, that something in you becomes clear.

That clarity rarely comes elegantly. Usually it comes through friction. Through overextension. Through realizing, too late, that you have been paying with parts of yourself you can’t easily recover.

And I don’t think there is anything shameful in that.

Maybe a boundary is just honesty that arrives a little late.

A lot of the time, the truth isn’t that we don’t want something. It’s that we don’t want it enough to keep exchanging ourselves for it. That’s a harder thing to admit, because it comes with loss. It means accepting that some things might remain partial, or distant, or unrealized, not because they were impossible, but because we were not willing to pay what they asked of us.

The same is true in other parts of life too.

If you want to go very far in your career, there may be a version of that path that asks for chronic stress, a narrowed life, and the quiet erosion of everything outside work. If you want love, that has its own cost too. Part of it is the long work of becoming more honest, more open, more capable of staying. Another part is accepting that no matter how sincere you are, you still cannot control whether someone will meet you there. You can offer your presence. You can try. But you can’t guarantee the outcome. That isn’t failure. That’s just part of the cost of caring.

Some things are not unattainable. They’re just expensive in ways we don’t always want to admit.

Lately I’ve also been thinking about that Nietzsche line, that if a person has a why, they can bear almost any how. I used to read it as something almost heroic. Now I think it also reveals something quieter. If there is a way of living I cannot bear anymore, maybe that tells me something about my why. Maybe it means that whatever I thought I wanted has not become important enough, or true enough, to justify the cost.

So maybe the deeper question in life isn’t simply what am I living for.

Maybe it’s this:

What kind of cost am I actually willing to bear?

What kind of pain, uncertainty, discipline, risk, loneliness, or surrender am I willing to take on for the life I say I want?

And when the answer is “not that,” can I be honest about that too?

Can I accept that every refusal has a shape? That every boundary closes something as much as it protects something?

We like to think we’re trapped by difficult choices. But I’m not sure that’s always true.

Sometimes we’re trapped by wanting everything without paying for everything. Sometimes we’re trapped by the fantasy that there is a way to keep all the doors open.

There usually isn’t.

At some point, growing up might just mean admitting this plainly:

there are things I could have had, but not without becoming someone I didn’t want to be, or living in a way I wasn’t willing to keep living.

And maybe that’s not failure.

Maybe that’s all maturity really is, knowing that every life costs something, and finally being honest about which costs are yours, and which are not.

In the end, maybe we are not defined by what we want.

Maybe we are defined by what we are willing to pay for, and what we are finally willing to let go of.
